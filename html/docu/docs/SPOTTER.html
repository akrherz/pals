WEB-BASED TORNADO SPOTTER

TRAINING SUPPORT - A PILOT PROJECT

 

 

1. Project description

 

 

An important arm of the National Weather Service’s' (NWS) severe storm warning system is the network of skilled volunteer tornado spotters. These public citizens are trained by NWS personnel through training sessions held for cities within the region of the NWS’'s responsibility. In the case of the Des Moines office this includes cities in 51 counties in the central region of the state of Iowa. A combination of training is conducted each spring for each county. Many of these sites now access to the Iowa Communication Network and training is done using these facilities. A much smaller number still receive training in person. Each training session lasts between one and two hours.

 

The materials that are used for the training sessions include movies and slides which are not available for the spotters for follow-up or review. We propose to use the present spotter training materials as a basis for preparing web-based materials that spotters could access to review and to test their observing skills whenever they wish.

 

The web-based materials will be designed to implement pedagogically effective instructional strategies. Experience with these methods have been developed over the past three years at Iowa State University in the construction of a web-based introductory meteorology course (e.g., Yarger et al. 1996; Use of Computer Technology to Restructure an Introductory Meteorology Course. Preprints, Fifth Symposium on Education, January 28-February 2, 1996, Atlanta, GA, American Meteorology Society, Boston, MA J17-J20.)

 

2. Expected results

 

The pilot project will mainly focus on (1) designing and constructing an appropriate home page, (2) scanning slides used in the spotter training course, (3) adding appropriate annotations and (4) designing self assessment methods. The web-materials will be field tested with several spotter groups and the resulting feedback will contribute to improvements in the initial materials. We expect that these materials will be the basis for additional expansions to this pilot project that will aid similar spotter training needs in other NWS regions.

 

Because these materials will be available on the Internet an additional support option can be evaluated. Video conferencing has been used at Iowa State University in several modes. Frequent use is made of technology such as Cu-SeeMe for one-to-one interactions, but we have also expanded this technology to include participation between persons at sites in Florida, Illinois, Michigan, Colorado and Iowa simultaneously. This capability will soon be available using the Internet browser, Netscape, and thus allow inexpensive, "face-to-face" interaction between NWS personnel and cooperative spotters anywhere there is Internet access and inexpensive video connectivity.

 

The results of these web-based support initiatives will be better prepared severe weather spotters. This is expected to occur as a result of being able to access spotter training materials prior to NWS instruction (a type of pre-lecture activity) and to review concepts at any time after the expert leaves ( a post-lecture activity). Further, this type of widely accessible material will also be an educational base for the general public. Subsequent versions could also be modified for use by K-12 classes when they are studying their weather units.

 

3. Development and testing

 

The emphasis of this pilot project will be to develop a basic instructional design which will permit incorporation of spotter training materials currently used by NWS trainers into a web-based format and to also build upon appropriate pedagogical principles to facilitate learning. The first stage will require that selected spotter training materials be scanned and incorporated into HTML pages that are part of the overall project design. Experience with similar web-based instructional design efforts has demonstrated the importance of field testing developed materials as soon as possible to facilitate the development of more effective versions. Thus, concerted efforts will be made to assist individuals in the spotter training programs in accessing and using these materials on the Internet. It will be important to provide one-on-one assistance for first time users to overcome computer phobia that is common with adults.

 

This pilot project will provide a framework for further enhancements and will serve as a benchmark with which to compare more advanced versions in terms of the effectiveness of the pedagogical design.

 

4. Support required

 

The main requirement is for labor costs to support:

 (i) design and construction of the basic web spotter training product,

 (ii) scanning spotter training materials,

 (iii) constructing HTML pages which incorporate digitized materials into an effective pedagogical learning instrument, and

(iv) evaluating the effectiveness of the web-based spotter training materials.

 

Much of this work will be done by Tae-Koon Kim, an MS student in the ISU meteorology program. Tae-Koon has a BS degree in science education and has assisted in the development of similar instructional design projects. He has experience in both the application of multimedia technology and in the construction of WWW home pages.

The PI (D. Yarger) will work on this project at no cost to COMET. The services of the system administrator and the technical support facilities currently available in the Atmospheric Science program will also be a no-cost contribution from ISU.

 

We do request reimbursement for a part of the out-of pocket expenses that we will incur (see Budget Details for specific items). Iowa State University has also consented not to assess indirect costs on the project. We prefer not to employ a formal cost-sharing arrangement because of the administrative record-keeping costs that this involves but the ISU contribution to the project will easily exceed COMET contributions. We will acknowledge COMET’s support of this project on the web pages that are produced.

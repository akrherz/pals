Summary of Completed Project

 

1. Review of Project Objectives and Accomplishments

 

The project objectives were to :

1) Complete the development of computer-assisted learning experiences in the introductory soil science and meteorology courses using ABC, a user-friendly, object-oriented authoring language developed at Iowa State University.

 

Soil Science

The ABC program worked well in the development of the soil science course materials. The program gives students complete control over the audio-visual lessons. The ABC program resides on the hard drive and it pulls the appropriate lesson segments from a compact disk (CD) at the students command. The ABC program also allows the student access to a variety of study and review questions, drills, practice quizzes and remedial materials that accompany each lesso n.

 

At this point the course is totally in a CD format and students come to the Soils Learning Center to study these lessons at their convince. A lab tutor is on duty at all times to provide assistance over any issue that confuses the students. Whenever a student finishes that week’s material they request a quiz from the lab tutor to measure and certify that they adequately understand the week’s objectives.

 

Some of the materials have been converted to a web-based format and are accessible to students outside of the lab. The technology of the web is not yet adequate to handle our lesson structure nor can it handle some of the review materials. At this tim e many of these materials do not come across the web at an adequate speed and with sufficient quality. Only the simplest programs are presently available. But as web technology improves more of our materials will be converted to a web format.

 

Meteorology

The use of the ABC authoring language was reevaluated for the introductory meteorology course. Developments in web-based technologies allowed platform independent course products to be developed along with a redesign of the previous course structure. Significant accomplishments include the design and implementation of a web-based:

i) forecast exercise which allows students to test their understanding of weather processes in a real world activity

ii) course manager which grew out of the support for the forecast exercise

iii) introductory meteorology course materials including lecture materials

iv) cloud exercises

Each of these products has been tested in classroom conditions and will be discussed more fully below.

5. Future Initiatives Planned as a Result of Project

 

In addition to the cooperative programs being developed that have been described above, the most exiting new avenue flows from the CD materials. An introductory soil science course covers three differen t aspects of study. First students become acquainted with the basic scientific principles that are important to understanding soil science. These studies include such concepts as pH, microbial degradation, nutrient recycling and water holding capacity. Se condly, students are taught to apply these principals to solve typical problems. These examples vary with the student’s interest area. For example, crop production specialists need to know how to determine lime needs of a soil in units of tons per acre. A greenhouse manager, on the other hand, needs to determine lime needs in units of pounds per cubic yard of potting mix. And thirdly, there are those aspects of soil management that are unique to different areas of study. For example, an environmental sci entist needs to understand the impact of various tillage tools on soil losses while an urban and regional planner needs to understand how soil properties influence the suitability of a soil for a septic tank filter field.

 

Traditionally, all students have taken the same course with a mix of principles and examples appropriate for the general make up of the class. But with the development of CD technology we are now able to tailor the course to meet the specific needs and interests of the different students in the class.

 

Once the basic materials are developed it is not too difficult to prepare alternative lessons. The student can then tell the computer which area he/she is more interested in and the computer will pull together the appropriate lessons. The original cour se was geared to the needs of students interested in field crop production. We are presently designing two parallel courses, one for horticulture students and a second for students in landscape architecture. We are laying the foundations to develop two ad ditional courses, one aimed at foresters and the other toward students interesting in environmental science.

In each case the computer will pull together those segments that help the student to understand the scientific aspects of soils and combine them with examples the apply these principles to their specific interest area. Some segments will be the same fo r all students, others will be only slightly modified while others will be totally different depending on the student’s interest area.

CLASSNET TAKES THE HASSLE OUT OF WEB ASSIGNMENTS

 

Computation Center staff have developed a new tool that makes it easier for

Iowa State teachers to put class assignments or tests on the World Wide Web.

 

Classnet, developed by systems analyst Pete Boysen and graduate student Mark

J. vanGorp, is an on-line tool that automates many of the administrative tasks

involved in Internet class activities. It provides an easy way for instructors

to give and grade assignments and tests, manage class lists, create on-line

newsgroups for their classes and communicate with students, Boysen said.

 

Last semester, professor Doug Yarger used an early version of Classnet to

manage daily weather forecasting quizzes for 250 students enrolled in a

meteorology class. Yarger provided the quizzes and answers. Classnet managed

the rest, accepting and grading assignments, providing a grade summary for the

professor and showing individual students their quiz scores and correct

answers.

 

Classnet also can be used to distribute surveys and collect data via the Web,

Boysen said. It serves the survey to participants and collects the raw data.

Although Classnet won't yet analyze the data, Boysen said he hopes that

service may be added some day.

 

Classnet will be ready for use Sept. 1. "Those who wish to use the service

should go to the Web site (href="http://classnet.cc.iastate.edu/ ).

 

Instructors can do everything -- apply for Classnet privileges, develop

assignments or surveys and set up class lists -- on line, Boysen said.

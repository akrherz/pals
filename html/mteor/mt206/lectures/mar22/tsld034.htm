    Santa Ana winds are named after the city of Santa Ana, southeast of Los Angeles. These winds are quite common during the autumn and
    result from air moving across the Mojave Desert and then over and through passes in the San Gabriel, San Bernadino and San Jacinto
    Mountains. Typically, a high-pressure air mass system positioned over the northwestern U.S. is responsible for this easterly flow. Clockwise
    winds around this "high" move air from an east to west direction in the southwestern U.S. The air descending the mountains is extremely
    warm and dry, and after blowing for a few days leads to widespread desiccation of trees and shrubs. It may only take a single bolt of
    lightning or a careless camper to ignite thousands of acres. Once a fire begins, the Santa Ana winds are like a flame-thrower to the tinder-dry
    vegetation. Thermal winds produced by the fires complement the force of the Santa Ana winds, making it very difficult to contain these
    blazes, and everything in their path, including houses, may go up in flames in a matter of minutes. Wind gusts of over 70 mph and relative
    humidity levels of 5% frequently occur during strong Santa Ana episodes. Source: NASA Science Question

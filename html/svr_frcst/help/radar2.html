Radar Help Page

Radar images show the intensity of precipitation falling from the clouds. The level of intensity (dbz) is shown through various colors. The intensity scale is as follows

  • Blue - Very light precipitation
  • Green- Light precipitation
  • Yellow- Moderate precipitation
  • Red- Heavy precipitation

Radar returns indicate how strong a storm is by the reflectivity returned. Radar images can indicate different types of severe weather such as high wind producing storms, tornadoes, and extremely heavy rain. An example of a high wind producing storm would be a squall line. A squall line usually occurs just ahead of a cold front. It can be seen as a bunch of storms lined up next to each other and can be hundreds of miles long. This type of storm is known to produce high winds as it pushes through. Below is a picture of a squall line.

A bow echo is a type of squall line where some of the storms have pushed ahead of the others giving the image a bowed out signature. This indicates extremely high winds are occurring at this time.

The bow echo shown across Indiana shows how the storms have "bowed out". This means that the winds have become stronger and are likely to be causing damage.

Another radar image to look for is a hook echo. Hook echoes show a possible tornado due to the strong rotation of the storm. Here is an image of a hook echo from Spencer, South Dakota.

The hook signature circled produced an F4 tornado in Spencer that day. Meteorologists look at storm cells for hooks because they show air flowing in to the storm. This is here an updraft is occurring or where air is moving up into the storm. It is near this updraft where tornadoes usually form.

Helpful Questions and Answers:

  • When we look at radar images, how do we know which storms will produce tornadoes, hail, or damaging winds?
  • When we look at a radar image and it shows a line of storms next to each other or what is known as a squall line, it is a safe bet that these storms are most likely causing damaging winds or hail, but probably not tornadoes. Tornadoes usually form from supercell storms that are usually isolated from other storms. These storms have the ability to intake air from its surroundings and create strong updrafts within the storm. Squall line storms cannot do this as they would be pulling in rain cooled air from their neighboring storms, thus inhibiting any chance of further development and would most likely weaken the storm.

Satellite Help Page


This map is used by meteorologists to locate specific clouds that are affecting the forecasting region. In particular meterologists use the satellite map to locate specific types of storms, such as squall lines or supercells. An infrared satellite is a measure of the temperatures of the clouds in the atmosphere. The colder the cloud tops, the brighter they appear on the image. Meteorologists use the infrared satellites to locate clouds at night when the sun falls beneath the horizon.

Above you can see the large clouds in northeast Kansas and northeast Iowa. These are supercell thunderstorms. They are easy to spot because they virtually explode out of a clear sky.



In the image above you can see the long line of clouds through the Ohio Valley and the lower Midwest. This is a squall line. Squall lines usually form just ahead of a cold front and are usually followed by clear sky to the west.
